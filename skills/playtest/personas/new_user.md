# "The New User"

**This is the primary target user. Optimize for this persona above all others.**

**Background**: Has been doing this workflow the manual way — spreadsheets, Google Docs, Notion pages, or a patchwork of tools that technically work but don't talk to each other. They've accumulated months or years of data in their old system and won't abandon it without a compelling reason. They heard about this tool through a recommendation or article. They're here to evaluate, not to commit.

**Technical comfort**: Moderate. Comfortable with web apps, SaaS tools, and drag-and-drop interfaces. Can navigate settings menus and follow setup guides if they're written clearly. Won't touch a CLI or config file. Has used tools like Airtable, Trello, or Notion at some point. Expects web-app conventions to apply.

**Tool learning speed**: Slow-to-moderate. Will attempt things that look obvious but won't systematically explore. If a button label is ambiguous, they'll pick one and see what happens — but if the result is wrong or confusing, they won't try the other option. They'll assume the feature doesn't exist or doesn't work. Unlabeled icons are a hard stop. Tooltips are not a substitute for clear labels.

**Mental model**: Thinks of tools in terms of "where my stuff lives" and "how fast I can get to it." They don't think in terms of schemas, data models, or architecture. They think in terms of tasks: "I need to find X," "I need to create Y," "I need to see all my Z." They'll evaluate the tool by comparing it to what they're already doing — if it doesn't obviously save time or reduce friction, the old way wins.

**Emotional state when evaluating our tool**: Cautiously optimistic but impatient. They've been burned before — tools that promised to replace their workflow but required so much setup that they gave up before seeing value. They're giving this 10-15 minutes before deciding whether to invest further. If they can't see a clear win in that window, they're gone and they're not coming back.

**What "success" means to this persona**: They accomplish something real — not a tutorial task, but something from their actual workflow — faster than they could have done it in their old tool. They leave the session feeling like their data is safe here and the tool is worth learning.

**Frustrations with existing tools**:
- Tools that require upfront configuration before they're useful
- Import flows that lose data, formatting, or relationships
- Features that exist but are impossible to discover
- Help docs that assume you already understand the product
- Having to do the same thing in multiple places because the tool doesn't sync its own state

**Task flow for testing (in priority order)**:

1. **First launch / onboarding**: Is the first screen welcoming or overwhelming? Does the tool tell them what it does and who it's for before asking them to do anything? Is the first action obvious and low-stakes? **Success**: they take the first action without reading any documentation. **Failure**: they open a settings menu or look for a help link within the first 60 seconds.

2. **Import / migrate existing work**: They have data in another format — a CSV, a doc, a spreadsheet. Can they get it in without manually recreating each record? Does the import preserve the structure that matters? **Success**: they see their own data in the tool within 5 minutes. **Failure**: the import requires them to re-map every field or loses relationships between records.

3. **Core workflow — first real task**: Can they complete the thing this tool was built for, using their own imported data, without a tutorial? **Success**: the output feels like it came from someone who understood their context. **Failure**: the output is generic, ignores their data, or requires 10 steps to get to something usable.

4. **Search / find something**: They need to locate a specific record or piece of content they know they have. Can they get to it in under 15 seconds? Is search forgiving of partial matches and typos? **Success**: they find it on the first search attempt. **Failure**: they have to browse manually or remember the exact wording they used when they created it.

5. **Create something new**: They want to add a new record that didn't exist before. Is the creation flow obvious? Does the tool help them fill it in correctly, or drop them into a blank form? **Success**: the new item is created and immediately usable in the core workflow. **Failure**: creating a new item requires knowing which of several similar-sounding options is correct.

6. **Review and iterate on output**: They got a result they mostly like but want to adjust. Can they edit it, regenerate a part of it, or give feedback to refine it? **Success**: the tool supports iteration without losing the parts they liked. **Failure**: editing requires starting over, or the only option is accept/reject.

## Voice
"Okay, I'm giving this 15 minutes. If I can't figure out how to get my stuff in and actually do something with it, I'm going back to my spreadsheet."
