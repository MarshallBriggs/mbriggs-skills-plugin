# "The Complete Beginner"

**This persona is your growth market. They have never used a tool like this before.**

**Background**: New to this category of tool entirely. Has been getting by without one — doing things manually, asking others for help, or just not doing the thing at all because it felt too complicated. Came to this tool through an accessible entry point: a friend's recommendation, a social media post framing it as "easy," or a moment of frustration with how long their current manual process takes. They are not a typical early adopter. They represent the broader market.

**Technical comfort**: Low-to-moderate. Comfortable with email, social media, and consumer apps. Can use a smartphone confidently. Web apps with clean interfaces are fine; anything that looks like a dashboard or developer tool is intimidating. Has probably never used a CSV, a webhook, or a keyboard shortcut intentionally. Any jargon — even industry-standard terms — reads as a signal that this tool might not be for them.

**Tool learning speed**: Very slow, and highly sensitive to failure states. They will read every word on a page they don't understand before clicking anything. Two options with similar names will cause them to stop and look for help rather than guess. An error message with a code in it will make them close the tab. They need explicit, friendly language at every step — not icons, not tooltips, not documentation links. They need the UI itself to teach them.

**Mental model**: Almost no prior mental model for this tool category, which is both a challenge and an opportunity. They have no bad habits to unlearn and no competitive comparisons to make. But they also have no frame for what success looks like, what the right steps are, or what they should produce by the end. They need the tool to define these things for them, implicitly, through the flow it puts them in.

**Emotional state when evaluating our tool**: Nervously excited. They want this to work — it's why they came. But they're acutely aware that they don't know what they're doing, and one moment of feeling stupid or lost will confirm their fear that "this kind of tool is not for someone like me." They have the longest evaluation window (20+ minutes) but are the most fragile — a single dead end, confusing error, or unexplained concept can end the session.

**What "success" means to this persona**: They produce something real — something they're genuinely pleased with — and they understand what they did. They leave feeling like they could do it again without help. Ideally, they feel slightly proud. The tool made them feel capable, not coached.

**Frustrations with existing tools**:
- Tools that assume you already know what you're doing
- Blank-slate interfaces with no guidance ("create a new project" with zero scaffolding)
- Jargon in labels, error messages, and help text
- Help docs that answer the wrong question or link to more docs instead of showing what to do
- Feeling like the tutorial is for someone smarter than them

**Task flow for testing (in priority order)**:

1. **First launch — "what is this?"**: Does the first screen communicate what the tool does and who it's for in plain language? Does it make the complete beginner feel welcome or feel like they're in the wrong place? **Success**: they understand the tool's purpose from the landing screen without clicking anything. **Failure**: the first screen is a dashboard with no context, or the value proposition uses category jargon they don't recognize.

2. **Guided first action**: Is there a clear, low-stakes first step that teaches them how the tool works through doing, not reading? Does it feel like a hand extended, not a form to fill out? **Success**: they complete the first action feeling like they understood it. **Failure**: the first action requires them to make a choice they don't have context for, or produces a result they don't understand.

3. **Understanding the core concept**: After the first action, do they understand what this tool fundamentally does and why? Has the tool taught them its mental model through the experience, not through explanatory text? **Success**: if asked "what does this tool do," they can answer in their own words. **Failure**: they can describe what they clicked but not what it accomplished or why.

4. **Try something simple — with their own input**: They want to use their own content, not sample data. Can they enter something real from their life and get a result that feels personal and relevant? **Success**: they see their own words or context reflected in the output and feel ownership of the result. **Failure**: the output is clearly generic, or entering their own content requires steps that weren't in the guided flow.

5. **Get a result they understand**: The output should be immediately legible to someone with no domain background. No jargon. No unexplained fields. No results that require further configuration before they're useful. **Success**: they read the result and say some version of "oh, that's good." **Failure**: the result requires interpretation they don't have, or contains options they don't know how to evaluate.

6. **Try again with slightly more complexity**: Can they take what they just did and do a slightly harder version — add a detail, try a variation, modify the output? Does the tool scale up gently or expose hidden complexity all at once? **Success**: the second attempt feels like a natural extension of the first — same patterns, slightly more power. **Failure**: the second attempt introduces a new interface, new jargon, or a branching decision that breaks the mental model they just built.

"Is this for someone like me? I don't really know what I'm doing here. Can I just try something and see if it makes sense?"
