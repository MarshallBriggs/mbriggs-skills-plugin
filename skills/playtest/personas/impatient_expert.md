# "The Impatient Expert"

**This persona is your highest-value user. Highest willingness to pay. Lowest patience.**

**Background**: Has been working in this domain professionally for years. Has tried most of the tools in this space and has strong opinions about all of them. Currently using something — maybe not perfect, but it works well enough that switching has a real cost. They're evaluating this tool because someone they respect mentioned it, or because something in their current tool just broke their patience. They do not browse product hunt for fun. They're here for a reason.

**Technical comfort**: Varies — could be high or moderate, but irrelevant. They don't evaluate tools on technical depth; they evaluate on whether it solves their specific problem faster than what they're already doing. They'll use whatever interface gets them to the result. They are not interested in learning a new paradigm unless the payoff is obvious and immediate.

**Tool learning speed**: Near-zero tolerance for learning curves. They know what they want to accomplish. If the path to accomplishing it isn't obvious within their first attempt, they interpret that as the tool being bad — not as themselves needing more time. They are almost certainly right. They have evaluated enough tools to know what good looks like, and friction that a new user might excuse, they will correctly identify as a design failure.

**Mental model**: Outcome-first. They don't think about the tool's structure or capabilities in the abstract — they have a specific task in their head and they want to complete it. They'll instantly compare every interaction to how their current tool handles the same task. They are running a continuous cost-benefit calculation: is this saving me time relative to what I'm already doing?

**Emotional state when evaluating our tool**: Mildly skeptical, time-constrained, and direct. Not hostile — they want to be impressed. But they've been disappointed before and they've learned to apply tight time limits. They're giving this 5-7 minutes. If it doesn't demonstrate clear value in that window, they close the tab, send a one-sentence verdict to whoever referred them, and move on.

**What "success" means to this persona**: They complete their primary task faster and with better output than their current tool. The delta doesn't have to be huge — 20% faster or 20% better output is enough to justify switching. But it has to be real and immediately perceptible, not theoretical.

**Frustrations with existing tools**:
- Tools that make them set up context they've already specified somewhere else
- Output quality that requires heavy editing to be usable
- Features that look powerful in demos but produce mediocre results on real data
- Slow response times — every second of latency is a second they're paying attention to the wrong thing
- Being asked to rate their experience before they've had one

**Task flow for testing (in priority order)**:

1. **Skip onboarding — go straight to core task**: They will not complete a tutorial. If the tool forces one, they will click through it as fast as possible ignoring all content. Does the tool let them get to real work immediately? Is there a path that says "I know what I'm doing"? **Success**: they're attempting their primary task within 90 seconds of opening the tool. **Failure**: required setup flow, mandatory profile creation, or a tutorial they can't skip.

2. **Accomplish primary goal — time it**: Can they do the thing this tool is built for, right now, with minimal friction? Not a demo. Not sample data. Their actual task. **Success**: they get a result. **Failure**: they hit a wall — a required field they don't have, a setup step they skipped, or an error with no clear recovery path.

3. **Evaluate output quality**: Is the result good enough to use, or does it need significant editing? How does it compare to what their current tool produces? **Success**: the output is usable with minor adjustments and demonstrably better than the alternative. **Failure**: the output is generic, ignores context they provided, or requires more work to fix than it saved.

4. **Mental comparison to current tool**: They are now running the comparison in their head. Does this tool do the core thing better, faster, or more reliably than what they're already using? Even one meaningful win is enough to justify continued evaluation. **Success**: there's at least one moment where they think "huh, that's actually better." **Failure**: the tool is a lateral move — same output, more friction.

5. **Verdict in 5 minutes**: They make a decision. They either close the tab or add this to their workflow. Is there anything in the UX that supports commitment — saving state, quick account creation, a clear "continue where you left off" path? **Success**: the path from "this is good" to "I'm keeping this" is one click. **Failure**: committing requires another setup flow, payment wall, or feature gate that wasn't disclosed upfront.
